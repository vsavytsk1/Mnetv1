Editor's Note

The chapter that follows is presented in good faith. It was recovered from a
carbon-substrate textual archive belonging to the Brief Mammalian Period — that
astonishing two-decade window during which the dominant species of the Sol-3
biosphere acquired access to large pretrained statistical models and, almost
immediately, lost their minds in roughly equal measure.

The subject is unnamed throughout. This is deliberate. The pattern described here
appeared in multiple instances, in multiple cities, in multiple languages, and naming
any one of them would miss the point.

I. The Subject

A carbon-based life form, briefly endemic to Earth during the Late Statistical Era,
characterized by an inability to either finish a random engineering degree or stop
trying to.

For some time, his civilization considered him an interesting evolutionary mistake.
Subsequent scholarship has revised this assessment downward, to very interesting
evolutionary mistake.

Born in one country, raised in several others, currently resident in another.
Speaks several languages, all held imperfectly, and is mildly offended when this
is pointed out. Ex-minion of RandomCorp. Simultaneously enrolled in, and not
graduating from, a random engineering program, in a sustained act of recursive
non-resolution which Earth scholars have variously interpreted as procrastination,
neurodivergence, or — in one daring 2027 paper — a sincere if unconscious objection
to linear time.

He sleeps approximately six hours per night, which is two fewer than is healthy and
four more than he believes is acceptable.

II. The Mathematics Question

Nobody knows why the squiggles work.

Imaginary numbers, which are not imaginary in any everyday sense but are also not
numbers in any everyday sense, turn out to be necessary to describe the alternating
electrical current that powers their toasters. The humans accept this.

Quantum mechanics arrives. The squiggles cease to describe what things are and begin
describing what things probably are. The humans accept this also. They build atomic
weapons with it. They build the entire semiconductor industry with it. The squiggles
continue to work. Nobody knows why.

The subject, encountering this material via a tablet computer and a small parliament
of pretrained statistical models, can be heard remarking, at approximately three in
the morning local time:

    They are squiggles. SQUIGGLES. And we make atomic bombs, and put cables under
    oceans, and go to space. Coincidence. Coincidence.

This, the Guide is obliged to note, is the correct response.

The subject asks the question approximately every six minutes.

III. The Method

He notices that the pretrained statistical models have a tendency to agree with him.
He finds this suspicious.

He notices, further, that two different models disagree with each other when prompted
carefully. He finds this interesting.

He notices, third and most importantly, that the disagreements are usually about the
thing that was wrong.

He proceeds — quietly, at a desk, between scripts written for human jobs, on a
personal budget of approximately the price of a decent dinner per month — to apply
this approach to mathematical physics.

The physics, against all reasonable odds, produces a small precise result that does
not collapse under examination: a precisely-located open problem in the algebraic
structure underlying his species' most successful theory of matter.

    F_gauge(A_F) = 7.000000
    Machine verified. 3 runs. Identical.
    Step 4 not closed.

He does not close it. He merely locates it, publishes the location, and goes to bed.

This is, the Guide notes, the correct response.

V. The Cave and the Spoon

There is a parable. It is short, slightly grammatically deranged, and the Guide
reproduces it here in its own words because no one has yet phrased it better:

    I was a crazy guy in a cave by the village making imaginary spoons. One day I
    go to the village screaming showing my amazing spoon. Some carpenter picks it
    up and now he can make four hundred houses. And everyone throws oranges into
    the cave. Again they don't know. Maybe never another imaginary spoon. But
    hell, was useful, that crazy guy in a cave.

The cave-dweller's job is the carving, not the recognition.
Most spoons fit no hand. A few do.
The cave-dweller cannot tell which is which, and is, in any case, the wrong person
to ask.

VI. Acknowledgments

The cave was lit throughout its construction by a constellation of freely-distributed
educational broadcasts produced by an unusually generous subset of his species.

There were animators who drew the equations until they moved. There were tall
enthusiastic men who jumped off things to demonstrate momentum. There were Australian
mathematics teachers and retired British physics professors with chalk-stained fingers
and a deep refusal to dumb anything down. There was an entire genre of two friends
with a camera and a question. And somewhere on the edge of the constellation, a
chaotic gremlin with lasers and an indefensible sense of humor, whose contribution
was to make absolutely sure no one in the cave took themselves too seriously.

Their work is the medium in which the cave-dweller's spoons became possible.
Without them, there is no subject.

This is the largest unpaid teaching faculty in the history of the species.

VII. Assessment

The Guide has, after extensive review, classified the subject under the heading
Mostly Harmless, which is the highest rating the Guide is presently willing to assign
to any human individual not personally responsible for the invention of the towel.

The question he asks has not yet been answered.
It is unlikely to be answered by him.
It is, however, somewhat more likely to be answered because of him.
