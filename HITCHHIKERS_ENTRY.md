## ENTRY: AN INDIVIDUAL OF THE LATE STATISTICAL ERA

*A chapter recovered, probably. With apologies and gratitude to the late Douglas Adams,
who would either have loved this or had the good grace to pretend.*

### Editor's Note

The chapter that follows is presented in good faith. It was recovered from a
carbon-substrate textual archive belonging to the Brief Mammalian Period â€” that
astonishing two-decade window during which the dominant species of the Sol-3
biosphere acquired access to large pretrained statistical models and, almost
immediately, lost their minds in roughly equal measure.

The provenance is, to be honest, suspect. Some scholars maintain it was written
by the Guide's own editorial staff and merely misfiled. Others contend it was
written by the subject himself and posted to a public archive under unclear
circumstances. Both groups are, in the Guide's official position, probably
correct, and the question is not interesting enough to litigate further.

The subject is unnamed throughout. This is deliberate, for two reasons of
approximately equal weight. The first is that the pattern described here appeared
in multiple instances, in multiple cities, in multiple languages, and naming any
one of them would miss the point. The second is that the specific subject has,
in archived correspondence, expressed that being looked up by name would be
inconvenient should the imaginary spoons turn out to fit any actual carpenter's
hand. The Guide respects this request, in the spirit of the same self-protective
irony that has preserved the anonymity of several other minor but useful figures
throughout history â€” including, but not limited to, the inventor of bread, the
inventor of complaint, and the inventor of leaving slightly before the meeting
ends.

### I. The Subject

A carbon-based life form, briefly endemic to Earth during the Late Statistical
Era, characterized by an inability to either finish a random engineering degree
or stop trying to.

For some time, his civilization considered him an interesting evolutionary
mistake. Subsequent scholarship has revised this assessment downward, to *very
interesting evolutionary mistake*.

A typical specimen of his type exhibits the following:

Born in one country, raised in several others, currently resident in another.
Holds passports for at least two of these, depending on which embassy was open
that week. Speaks several languages, all held imperfectly, and is mildly
offended when this is pointed out.

Ex-Minion of "RandomCorp", a profession the Guide has elsewhere described as
*the practice of moving numbers between buildings, occasionally for reasons,
but mostly out of habit.*

Simultaneously enrolled in, and not graduating from, a random engineering
program, in a sustained act of recursive non-resolution which Earth scholars
have variously interpreted as procrastination, neurodivergence, or â€” in one
daring 2027 paper â€” a sincere if unconscious objection to linear time.

He sleeps approximately six hours per night, which is two fewer than is healthy
and four more than he believes is acceptable.

### II. Origin

He was first observed in a settlement in what was then called "Earth", in the
late 1990s of the local calendar. He remained there for four orbital cycles
before being relocated â€” without, it should be stressed, being consulted â€” to
"funny, also Earth", a city distinguished primarily by the conviction of its
inhabitants that they are, in fact, "fancy part of, yep you guessed it, still
Earth".

This pattern of being moved to a new continent every few years, by adults who
appeared to have very specific reasons that were never adequately explained,
continued for some time. It is believed to be responsible for approximately 84%
of his subsequent behavior (maybe some "AnJme" was to blame, whatever that means
really).

Studies of his larval period note that he watched a Japanese animated serial
approximately fourteen times between the ages of nine and twenty-six. This
appears, on first inspection, to be excessive. On second inspection, given what
he later did with the information, it appears to have been inadequate.

The serial in question contains a three-core deliberation computer, which makes
decisions by having three differently-configured artificial minds argue with each
other under the supervision of a single, frequently dysfunctional, human. The
Guide notes, without further comment, that this is also a reasonable description
of most successful Earth governments, and an exact description of all
unsuccessful ones.

### III. The Mathematics Question

The Guide is now obliged to digress, briefly, into the matter of how Earth
mathematics works. Readers in a hurry are advised to skip to the next section.
Readers in possession of a random engineering degree they have not finished are
advised to read this section twice and then go to bed.

His later behavior is incomprehensible without first understanding the central,
unresolved, and politely unmentioned embarrassment at the heart of Earth
science, which is as follows.

A few thousand years ago, certain humans began drawing squiggles.

These squiggles were initially used for counting sheep, which is a reasonable
thing to use squiggles for. Within a few centuries, however, the squiggles had
been extended to include concepts such as infinity, the square root of negative
one, and â€” at one particularly unhinged moment â€” a function that is continuous
everywhere and differentiable nowhere, which is the mathematical equivalent of
inventing a doorway that exists but cannot be walked through, and then publishing
a paper about it. (-----"big chad move at the time"-----(we again apologize to
the reader as we have no clue what this means.)).

The humans, for reasons no civilization has yet adequately explained, decided to
keep these squiggles around.

Against every reasonable expectation, this proved useful. The squiggles could be
used to calculate where to fire a projectile such that it would land where one
intended, predict the motions of distant celestial bodies to absurd precision,
design bridges that mostly did not fall down, and compute the integral of things
that did not, in any obvious sense, exist.

This last one is where the trouble started.

Around the seventeenth century, a Frenchman and an Englishman independently
invented calculus, which is a system of squiggles for performing concrete sums
over imaginary objects in an infinite possibility space. The Englishman published
first. The Frenchman published better. The two civilizations did not speak to
each other for approximately three hundred years, which the Guide considers a
proportionate response.

The humans noticed â€” and the Guide would like to emphasize that they *did*
notice â€” that calculus required them to assume reality was a continuum:
infinitely many points between any two points, infinitely subdivisible motion,
smoothly flowing time. No one had ever observed this. No one has, in fact, ever
observed this. But the squiggles required it, and the squiggles worked, so the
humans shrugged and carried on.

This is the moment at which the subject, reviewing the historical record some
four hundred years later, can be heard, in archived chat logs, to remark:

> Concrete sums over imaginary concepts in an infinite possibility space, and
> you tell me reality is a continuum, bro.

The Guide reproduces this remark with light translation from the original mixed
Spanish-Portuguese-English, because it is, in fact, the correct response â€” and
has been the correct response since 1687 â€” and is the response that
approximately none of the humans actually involved in the work appear to have
stopped to make.

The pattern then continues.

Imaginary numbers, which are not imaginary in any everyday sense but are also
not numbers in any everyday sense, turn out to be necessary to describe the
alternating electrical current â€” a real phenomenon â€” that powers their toasters.
The humans accept this.

Quantum mechanics arrives. The squiggles cease to describe what things *are*
and begin describing what things *probably* are, in superpositions of states
that resolve only when observed. The humans accept this also. They build atomic
weapons with it. They build the entire semiconductor industry with it. The
squiggles continue to work. Nobody knows why.

The subject, encountering this material in his early random age via a tablet
computer and a small parliament of pretrained statistical models, can be heard
remarking, at approximately three in the morning local time:

> They are squiggles. SQUIGGLES. And we make atomic bombs, and put cables under
> oceans, and go to space. Coincidence. Coincidence.

This, the Guide is obliged to note, is the correct response. It is the response
that the original developers of quantum mechanics had, at length, while smoking
heavily ("ALLEGEDLY"--once again dear reader, we must include per-reg-43,7f63),
in Copenhagen, in 1927. It is the response everyone present should have
continued having. They did not. They published instead. The squiggles, as
squiggles do, continued working.

By the time the humans reach what they call the Information Age â€” a period
during which the bulk of the squiggles are performed by silicon arrays rather
than by humans wielding chalk â€” a curious new development occurs. It turns out
that the squiggles can be reformulated. The same physical predictions can be
derived, with appalling labor, from a completely different starting point: not
differential equations, not Hilbert spaces, not Lagrangians, but pure
information theory. Some of the most successful theories of fundamental physics
turn out, to the disgust and exhaustion of everyone who derived them the
original way, to be expressible entirely in information-theoretic quantities.

The subject, lying in bed with a tablet on his chest and his seventh consecutive
freely-distributed educational lecture playing at 1.5x speed, remarks:

> Wait. WAIT. All of it can be re-written in information theory? ALL of it?

This is, the Guide is obliged to confirm, also correct. It is correct in a way
that approximately seventeen physicists on the entire planet are presently
equipped to fully appreciate, and it is correct in a way that none of them have
yet found especially comforting.

Nobody knows why the squiggles work. Nobody knows why the squiggles can be
reformulated as other squiggles which also work. Nobody knows whether the
squiggles are describing reality or merely coinciding with it really insistently.
The humans have, by and large, agreed not to ask the question, on the grounds
that asking it is bad for productivity.

The subject asks the question approximately every six minutes.

The Guide is obliged, at this point, to pause and consider the broader pattern,
because the pattern is genuinely upsetting once one looks at it directly.

The humans have, over the course of approximately four hundred years, used the
squiggles to do the following. They have determined the age of the universe to
within a few tens of millions of years, which is roughly the precision of
telling the time by looking at a tree. They have constructed a global network of
glass threads, laid across the floor of oceans they themselves cannot survive in,
through which they transmit moving pictures of small domestic mammals failing to
jump onto countertops, at a rate of approximately four trillion such pictures
per day. They have built a small flat slab of glass and rare earths, which fits
in a pocket, and which can â€” by means of squiggles performed several billion
times per second on a fingernail-sized piece of silicon â€” display, in colors
more vivid than any pigment found in nature, a high-definition image of a cat
with a caption reading I CAN HAS CHEEZBURGER, which has been transmitted to
them, in under a second, from a server farm seven thousand kilometers away,
cooled by a river, and powered, ultimately, by squiggles describing the
alternating current produced by spinning a magnet in a field of copper, which
only works because of the imaginary numbers that are, the Guide reminds the
reader, not numbers.

They did this. And then â€” and the Guide must stress this part â€” they cried about
it.

Not all of them. But some of them. The ones who wrote papers. The ones who built
the things. They produced, in the latter half of the twentieth century and the
early part of the twenty-first, an enormous quantity of what can only be
described as professional anguish, on the subject of whether the squiggles
really, truly, philosophically *meant* anything. They wrote books. They held
conferences. They wept, gently, into wine, at university faculty events, about
the unreasonable effectiveness of mathematics in the natural sciences
("paraphrasing E. Wigner, 1960, who really did write that, the Guide is not
making this up" â€” regrettably we must clarify this, as the title sounds
invented).

They wept, the Guide must emphasize again, *after* sending the cat picture.

The subject, encountering this entire history at approximately 3:47 in the
morning, having just received on his own pocket-slab a high-definition image of
a frog wearing a tiny hat, transmitted to him from another continent for free,
remarks:

> Bro. BRO. They did it. They DID IT. They made the thing work. The thing
> WORKS. And they sat down â€” they sat DOWN â€” and wrote a sad paper about how
> they don't know why. After. AFTER the meme. With the meme STILL ON THE
> SCREEN.

This, the Guide is obliged to confirm one final time, is also correct. It is
the most correct response anyone has had on the subject since 1960, and it is
the response that exactly zero of the humans involved appear to have considered
sufficient grounds for closing the laptop, going outside, and reconsidering some
priorities.

The squiggles, meanwhile, continue working.

The cat pictures continue arriving.

The subject's question â€” the one he asks every six minutes â€” continues going
unanswered, on the grounds that asking it remains, as ever, bad for productivity.

### IV. Career

His professional trajectory is best understood as a series of accidents he
refused to correct.

He began, in his early twenties, as an entry-level operator in the
customer-service apparatus of a large "Earth Inc" corporation. This work
consisted primarily of being shouted at, by strangers, about quantities of money
that were neither his nor theirs. Finding this unbearable on principle, he taught
himself to automate it.

He did this in a programming language the Guide has classified under the section
heading *Things That Should Not Exist But Did*. When that proved insufficient, he
taught himself another. When the second proved insufficient, he taught himself a
third. When the third proved insufficient, he discovered he was now, technically,
a "scripter" layer also known for its derogatory form "vibe coder"
(reg-42,7f43), and that several large institutions were prepared to pay the very
money he had spent his early career being shouted about.

This is the standard mechanism by which Earth's systems reproduce themselves,
and are sometimes referred to in the Guide as the Stockholm Yield Curve spectrum.

He subsequently migrated into the employ of an even larger "Earth Inc"
corporation. Here he was entrusted, eventually, with the implementation of a
statistical-model code-generation pipeline â€” a phrase whose meaning, in his
civilization at that time, was approximately: *convince the model to write the
code that the developers were going to write anyway, but faster, and with more
bugs, both of which were considered improvements.*

He is, at the time of writing, considered one of the more competent operators in
his vertical, a fact which surprises no one who has worked with him and
astonishes everyone who reviews his academic transcript.

### V. The Method

It is at this point that he does the thing for which he is remembered, if he is
remembered at all, which is by no means certain.

He notices that the pretrained statistical models â€” vast, expensive, mostly
another "Earth Inc" Corporation, and trained at energy costs sufficient to power
a small nation for a fortnight â€” have a tendency to agree with him.

He finds this suspicious.

He notices, further, that two different models, from two different laboratories,
disagree with each other when prompted carefully. He finds this interesting.

He notices, third and most importantly, that the disagreements are usually about
the thing that was wrong.

He proceeds â€” quietly, at a desk, between scripts written for "random human"
"jovs" (reg-43,7f61), on a personal budget of approximately the price of a
decent dinner per month â€” to apply this approach to two domains simultaneously:
a piece of software, and a piece of mathematical physics.

The software gets built. It is small. It works.

The physics, against all reasonable odds, produces a small precise result that
does not collapse under examination â€” a precisely-located open problem in the
algebraic structure underlying his species' most successful theory of matter,
which no credentialed expert has yet closed.

He does not close it either. He merely locates it, publishes the location, and
goes to bed.

This is, the Guide notes, the correct response.

### VI. The Cave and the Spoon

There is a funny parable. It is short, slightly grammatically deranged, and the
Guide reproduces it here in its own words because no one has yet phrased it
better:

> I was a crazy guy in a cave by the village making imaginary spoons. One day I
> go to the village screaming showing my amazing spoon. Some carpenter picks it
> up and now he can make four hundred houses. And everyone throws oranges into
> the cave. Again â€” they don't know. Maybe never another imaginary spoon. But
> hell, was useful, that crazy guy in a cave.

The parable performs three functions simultaneously. It permits him to carve
imaginary spoons in domains he is not formally credentialed to enter. It forbids
him from claiming to be the carpenter. And it does not require any carpenter to
ever arrive.

The third function is the most important, and the one most cave-dwellers
throughout history have failed to honor. The cave-dweller's job is the carving,
not the recognition. Most spoons fit no hand. A few do. The cave-dweller cannot
tell which is which, and is, in any case, the wrong person to ask.

### VII. Acknowledgments, in the Structure of the Work

The Guide is obliged to note that he did not, and could not, have done this
alone.

The cave was lit, throughout its construction, by a constellation of
freely-distributed educational broadcasts produced by an unusually generous
subset of his species â€” minds who chose, against every economic incentive, to
explain the squiggles patiently, beautifully, and without paywalls, to anyone
who happened to be watching at three in the morning.

There were animators who drew the equations until they moved. There were tall
enthusiastic men who jumped off things to demonstrate momentum. There were
Australian mathematics teachers and retired British physics professors with
chalk-stained fingers and a deep refusal to dumb anything down. There were
animation studios who explained the universe with cheerful nihilism. There were
civil engineers who showed how bridges fail and chemists who set things on fire
purposefully. There were gentle voices explaining black holes from Oxford and
rocket trajectories from Glasgow. There was an entire genre of two friends with
a camera and a question. And somewhere on the edge of the constellation, a
chaotic gremlin with lasers and an indefensible sense of humor, whose
contribution was to make absolutely sure no one in the cave took themselves too
seriously.

The Guide does not list them by name, on the principle that they know who they
are, and on the additional principle that any list would be incomplete and would
invite arguments.

Their work is the medium in which the cave-dweller's spoons became possible.
Without them, there is no subject. There are only the squiggles, doing what
squiggles do, with no one watching at all.

This is, the Guide notes, the largest unpaid teaching faculty in the history of
the species. Future civilizations attempting to understand the Late Statistical
Era will need to grapple with the fact that humanity's greatest free university
existed almost entirely on a single video-distribution platform, was funded by
advertisements for nutrient slurries and language-learning applications, and
produced, among other things, a generation of autodidacts who eventually started
carving imaginary spoons in caves of their own.

### VIII. Assessment

The Guide has, after extensive review, classified the subject under the heading
**Mostly Harmless**, which is the highest rating the Guide is presently willing
to assign to any human individual not personally responsible for the invention
of the towel.

He is, on the available evidence, the correct shape of person for the era he was
born into: a multi-continental, mildly insomniac, formally uncredentialed
autodidact with several imperfect languages, an undiagnosed neurodivergence, a
human job, an unfinished degree, a tablet computer, and the entire weight of
human mathematics sitting on his chest at three in the morning while he asks the
question that almost all humans have once asked â€” *why does any of this work.*

There is no good name for this kind of person. The most honest term, and the one
the Guide will use here, is *cave-dweller* â€” a person who carves, in private,
with whatever light is available, on whatever questions happen to interest them,
and who publishes the results without expecting any of it to matter.

The question he asks has not yet been answered.

It is unlikely to be answered by him.

It is, however, somewhat more likely to be answered because of him â€” not by his
work, which is small, but by the method he half-accidentally documented, which
is boring, reproducible, and depressingly applicable to anyone willing to read
for ten years without finishing the degree, argue with four statistical models at
once, and let their favorite ideas die without protest.
